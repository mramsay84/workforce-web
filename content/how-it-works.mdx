## Phase 1: You Describe the Project

**Phase Headline:** Start with a brief — no technical setup required.

You describe what needs to be done in plain language. No workflow configuration, no Python, no schema definitions, no agent orchestration logic. You write what you need; the platform figures out how to staff it and execute it.

The planning consultant will ask clarifying questions to sharpen the brief before designing the team. A well-articulated brief produces meaningfully better results — but the platform is designed to help you get there, not to require perfection upfront.

**What a brief looks like:**

> "Build a go-to-market plan for our Series B product launch — targeting enterprise HR teams, launching in Q3. We need ICP definition, a messaging framework, positioning against [category], channel strategy, and a launch sequence with asset dependencies."

That is a complete Workforce brief. No workflow design. No agent selection. No dependency mapping. You describe the project goal, the constraints, and the expected deliverables. The planner handles the rest.

**What happens next:** The planning consultant reviews your brief, designs the team, and proposes an execution plan. You do not need to do anything further until the proposal arrives for your approval.

## Phase 2: The Plan Is Proposed — You Approve It

**Phase Headline:** Before work starts, you approve the plan.

This is the most important phase to understand. No work begins until you have reviewed and approved a structured execution proposal. This is not a formality — it is the mechanism that keeps you in control of what the platform does on your behalf.

The planning consultant reviews your brief and produces a proposal that includes:

- **Team structure** — which leads are assigned and which specialist types each lead will hire
- **Phased execution plan** — what gets done in what order, with dependencies made explicit
- **Expected deliverables per phase** — what each team produces and when
- **Clarifying questions** — anything the planner needs resolved before execution begins

You review the proposal. If it looks right, you approve it. If something needs adjustment — a scope change, a different approach, a reordered phase — you push back and the planner revises. Nothing executes until you sign off.

> **You approve the plan. The team executes. You review what matters.**

The proposals system is not a sign-off formality. It is the structural guarantee that high-stakes decisions never happen without your direction. A project does not begin until you have explicitly confirmed that the plan matches your intent.

## Phase 3: The Team Executes — In Parallel

**Phase Headline:** Specialists work in parallel — each in their domain.

Once the plan is approved, leads spawn their specialist teams and execution begins. This is where Workforce's architecture produces its structural advantage: parallel workstreams, each run by domain specialists, with leads owning quality in their area.

**How the team is structured:**

Every project runs a lead-specialist hierarchy. A Lead owns a domain — backend engineering, legal analysis, market research, content production. Leads hire the right specialists for their workstream. Specialists receive scoped tasks — they have context on what they need, not on the full project. This is intentional: specialization produces depth; breadth produces generalism.

**Parallel execution in practice:**

An investment memo project runs a market research team, a financial analysis team, and a legal risk review team simultaneously — not in sequence. Each team produces its domain deliverable. A synthesis lead then assembles the integrated package. What takes a consulting team three sequential weeks, Workforce runs in parallel workstreams.

**Context persistence:**

Agents save their work between sessions. Projects do not lose state. A lead picks up exactly where the team left off. Long projects are not constrained by session limits.

**Domain-specific quality standards:**

Each work mode applies domain-appropriate quality standards — built into every agent in that mode:

- **Legal:** GREEN/YELLOW/RED risk tiering on every clause reviewed. RED items are flagged for mandatory human review. Every output carries "analysis, not legal advice" framing.
- **Research:** Evidence grading — primary vs. secondary, peer-reviewed vs. informal. Hypothesis tracking logs competing evidence explicitly. The Think Tank mode adds an explicit critique phase: agents challenge each other's findings before synthesis.
- **Finance:** Double-entry validation enforced. Every number traced to a source document. Period awareness applied throughout.
- **Development:** File locking prevents concurrent conflicts. No specialist self-merges — all commits go through lead review. Playwright E2E tests run on every build. Lighthouse audits confirm performance and accessibility thresholds.
- **Compliance:** Findings-only output — no code changes, no direct fixes. Severity-rated findings (Critical / High / Medium / Low) with remediation guidance.

The platform knows what "good" looks like in each domain. You do not have to define it.

## Phase 4: Review Cycles, Then Delivery

**Phase Headline:** Nothing closes without review.

**The internal review layer:**

Quality is enforced by the platform, not hoped for. Before any specialist task closes, the relevant lead reviews and approves the work. This is architectural — it cannot be skipped. No specialist self-merges in development. No research output ships without the lead reviewing the evidence citations. No content piece is finalized without editorial sign-off.

In Research and Think Tank modes, a structured critique phase runs before synthesis: agents are explicitly tasked with challenging each other's findings, identifying contradictions, and stress-testing conclusions before the synthesis specialist assembles the final deliverable. Competing hypotheses are tested, not averaged.

**What you receive at delivery:**

A structured deliverable package — not a dump of files. What that package looks like depends on the work mode:

- **Research deliverable:** A structured literature review, an evidence grading table (primary, secondary, peer-reviewed, informal), a hypothesis tracking log with supporting and contradicting evidence mapped, and a synthesis document. PRISMA methodology applied where relevant.
- **Development deliverable:** Working, deployed code at a live preview URL, a full automated test suite, a Lighthouse performance and accessibility audit, and technical documentation for the codebase.
- **Legal deliverable:** A clause-by-clause analysis with GREEN/YELLOW/RED risk tiers, negotiation recommendations on YELLOW items, and escalation flags on RED items. "Analysis, not legal advice" framed explicitly throughout.
- **Marketing deliverable:** A complete GTM package — ICP definitions, positioning framework, competitive differentiation map, messaging framework, channel strategy with prioritized mix, launch calendar with asset dependencies.

**What if something needs correction:**

The platform supports revision cycles. If a deliverable needs adjustment — a scope clarification, a revised approach, an additional domain element — you push back, the lead routes the revision, and specialists correct. The proposals system applies to mid-project decision points as well as the initial plan.
